Deceptive Humor: Understanding the Humor that Misleads!

Humor is powerful — it brings people together, makes us laugh, and brightens days. But what if some humor hides a darker secret? What if laughter becomes a mask for misinformation that spreads quietly and dangerously? Welcome to the world of Deceptive Humor.

A Story That Says It All

During the peak of the COVID-19 pandemic, memes started flooding the internet. One of them read:

“Ch*na products don’t last long — but this virus did. Well played, Ch*na.”

At first glance, it seems like a sarcastic joke. But beneath that humor lies a harmful stereotype and a veiled conspiracy. Such content blends entertainment with dangerous misinformation, leading people to internalize falsehoods — sometimes without even realizing it. That’s the danger of Deceptive Humor.

What Makes Deceptive Humor Different?

Deceptive Humor is quite different from traditional humor or the types of humor typically explored in current research. In existing literature, humor and misinformation are usually treated as two separate domains. But what if they come together?

That’s what Deceptive Humor is about — when humor is used as a mask to hide misinformation. On the surface, it appears as satire or a harmless joke targeting public figures, countries, or communities. But its true intent is to spread bias, false claims, or harmful narratives in a subtle, laughable package.

When people are repeatedly exposed to such content — even indirectly — they may start believing it as truth. There have been multiple incidents where such "jokes" have fueled hate, division, and even real-world harm. For instance, during COVID-19, there was a surge in hate toward people associated with Ch*na — all amplified by these seemingly innocent memes.

Identifying and mitigating Deceptive Humor is a timely and necessary area of research — and one that is still underexplored.
